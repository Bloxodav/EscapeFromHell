You can find similar cosmogenic myths among an array of other northern peoples (Mansi, Udmurt, Mari) among others. Even Indians of the Great Plains have the parallel structure: endless waters, mud from the bottom, that will become earth, two animals, or entities, or even brothers, their conflict, and scourge. Only specifics differ. The motive of two opposites and the contrast between them can be seen in just about any culture.\n\nThe legend I heard from the Altai shaman is a classical example of a dualistic myth. These myths describe the cosmos as a unity of opposite entities, phenomena. These stories reflect the views on what is considered helpful or harmful for the specific group, tribe, nation. Opposites can merge and create something new. For example, in Chinese mythology Yin and Yang complement each other and gravitate towards each other, creating balance and harmony in the universe.\n\nIt is curious that one can find echoes of archaic dualistic beliefs in a plethora of new and modern belief systems.
